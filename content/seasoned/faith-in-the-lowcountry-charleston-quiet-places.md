Charleston in the summer is a lot. Cruise crowds on Market Street, a wait at every brunch spot, traffic backed up over the bridge. We love this city, but some days you just need to get somewhere still.

The good news is the Lowcountry is full of still. You just have to know where to look. Here are a handful of the places we go when we need to slow down, pray, or just remember that God made all of this on purpose.

## The marsh at low tide

There's something about the pluff mud and the tall grass when the water pulls back that quiets your brain down. The birds work the flats, the light goes gold, and everything smells like the ocean deciding what to do next. We'll park somewhere off the causeway, roll the windows down, and just sit for a while. It's hard to stay anxious watching the tide do the same faithful thing it's done every day for thousands of years.

*"He makes me lie down in green pastures, he leads me beside still waters."* — Psalm 23:2. The Lowcountry has its own version of that verse.

## A pier at sunrise

Many people know the beach at sunset. Fewer know it at sunrise. Get out to Folly or Sullivan's early enough and you'll basically have the whole thing to yourself — a couple of surfers, an old guy with a fishing rod, and the sun coming up over the water like it's the first time it's ever done it.

## Under the Angel Oak

If you've never stood under the Angel Oak on Johns Island, put it on the list. It's somewhere around four or five hundred years old, with limbs so long they rest on the ground and grow back up again. You walk under it and you get small in the best way. It's been alive since before this country existed, quietly doing what it was made to do, and it makes your Tuesday problems feel a little more manageable.

Creation has a way of preaching. Romans 1 says as much — that what can be known about God is written into the things he made. The Angel Oak is a pretty good sermon.

## Pitt Street Bridge, Mount Pleasant

The old bridge in the Old Village doesn't go anywhere anymore — it just stretches out into the harbor and stops. Which turns out to be perfect. You can walk to the end, watch the boats, catch the skyline across the water, and let your shoulders come down from around your ears. Locals walk their dogs here at dusk. It's the kind of ordinary, unhurried place that faith actually grows in.

## Wherever you already are

Here's the honest part: you don't need a scenic overlook to meet God. Some of the best quiet we've found was in a parked car in a grocery store lot, or on the back porch before the coffee kicked in. The Lowcountry just makes it easier to notice — the marsh, the tide, the enormous old trees — because it's all so plainly *made.*
