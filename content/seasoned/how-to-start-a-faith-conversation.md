If you've ever wanted to talk about your faith but froze because you didn't want to be *"that person"* — the one who turns every conversation into a sermon — you're not alone. Most of us feel the tension. We love Jesus, we want others to know Him, and we're terrified of being pushy, awkward, or preachy.

Here's the good news: the most meaningful faith conversations rarely start with a pitch. They start with a person. At Salted Soul, our whole heart is captured in one line of Scripture:

> "Let your conversation be always full of grace, seasoned with salt, so that you may know how to answer everyone." — Colossians 4:6

Notice it doesn't say *seasoned with pressure.* Salt adds flavor. It makes people thirsty. That's the goal — not to win an argument, but to leave someone a little more curious about the hope you carry. Here are five ways to do exactly that.

## 1. Start with curiosity, not a conclusion

Preachy conversations begin with an answer. Great conversations begin with a question. Instead of leading with what someone *should* believe, get genuinely curious about what they *do* believe — and why. Ask about their story. Ask what gives them hope on a hard week. People can tell the difference between being a project and being a person, and curiosity always disarms.

## 2. Let your life ask the question first

Some of the best faith conversations don't start with words at all. They start when someone notices something *different* about you — the way you handle stress, the way you treat a server, the small cross stitched on the hat you're wearing — and they ask about it. Your job isn't to force the door open. It's to live in a way that makes people want to knock.

That's actually why we make what we make. A design that sparks a "hey, what's that about?" is a conversation waiting to happen — no cold open required.

## 3. Ask better questions than "Do you go to church?"

"Do you go to church?" gets you a yes or a no and a dead end. Try questions that invite a real story:

- "Did you grow up around faith at all? What was that like?"
- "What's something you're hopeful about right now?"
- "Has anyone ever prayed for you before?"

Open questions signal that you care about *them*, not about scoring a point — and they give the Holy Spirit a lot more room to work than a checklist ever will.

## 4. Listen twice as much as you talk

Here's the counterintuitive part: the most powerful thing you can do in a faith conversation is often to stop talking. When someone feels truly heard, walls come down. You earn the right to be honest about your own faith not by out-arguing them, but by out-loving them. Listen for the ache underneath the story — everyone is carrying something — and you'll usually find the exact place grace wants to land.

## 5. Leave the door open, not a decision on the table

You are not responsible for the outcome. You're responsible for the invitation. End conversations in a way that keeps the friendship warm: *"I'd genuinely love to talk more about this sometime,"* or *"Can I be praying for you this week?"* No ultimatums, no closing pitch. A door left open gets walked through far more often than a door someone feels shoved through.

## Grace, seasoned with salt

Sharing your faith was never meant to feel like a performance. It's just what happens when the hope inside you spills naturally into the way you live, listen, and love. Season your conversations with a little salt — grace first, curiosity always — and watch how many doors quietly open.
